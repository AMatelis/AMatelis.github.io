Module 01

How to Read a Scientific Paper

Estimated time: 25-30 minutes  |  No prerequisites required

What you will be able to do after this

Open any research paper in biology, medicine, or AI and extract the information that actually matters without getting lost in jargon.

Why This Skill Matters

Most high schoolers who want to do research have never been taught how to actually read a paper. They open one, hit a wall of jargon in the abstract, and close the tab. That is not a knowledge problem. It is a strategy problem.

Scientists do not read papers the way you read a textbook, front to back, word for word. They use a specific order and ask specific questions at each stage. Once you learn the strategy, papers stop being intimidating and start being one of the most useful tools you have.

This is also the first skill you will need if you ever cold email a professor, apply to a research program, or start your own independent project. You cannot contribute to a field you cannot read.

Part 1: The Anatomy of a Paper

Every scientific paper, whether it is about cancer diagnostics, AI, or neuroscience, follows the same basic structure. Learn this once and it applies everywhere.

Title

Tells you the topic and usually hints at the finding. Pay attention to qualifiers like "a novel approach" or "preliminary evidence" because these tell you how confident the authors are.

Abstract

A 150 to 300 word summary of the entire paper. Contains the question, methods, main result, and conclusion compressed into one paragraph. Dense but important.

Introduction

Explains the background and why the research question matters. Usually ends with a clear statement of what the study is trying to do.

Methods

Describes exactly how the study was conducted. What data was used, what experiments were run, what statistical tests were applied. This section is what separates real science from opinion.

Results

Reports what was found, usually with figures, tables, and statistics. No interpretation here, just data.

Discussion

Interprets the results. What do the findings mean? How do they compare to previous work? What are the limitations?

Conclusion

The takeaway. Usually one to two paragraphs.

References

Every claim in the paper is backed by a citation. This section is a goldmine because it is a reading list for going deeper on anything that interests you.

Part 2: The Right Order to Read a Paper

Here is the secret: do not start at the beginning. Professional researchers read papers in this order:

Step 1 - Title and Abstract (2 minutes)

Ask yourself: what question are they trying to answer? What did they find? Is this relevant to what I am looking for? If the answer to the last question is no, stop here. Researchers read dozens of abstracts for every paper they actually read in full. This is normal and efficient, not lazy.

Step 2 - Figures and Tables (5 minutes)

Before reading a single paragraph of text, flip through every figure. A well-written paper tells its entire story through its figures. Look at the axes, the labels, the trends. Ask yourself what they are showing you and whether it supports the claim in the abstract. This step alone will tell you 80 percent of what the paper contains.

Step 3 - Introduction (5 minutes)

Now that you know the answer, read the introduction to understand the question. Pay attention to the last paragraph because it almost always states the specific aim of the paper in plain language.

Step 4 - Results (10 minutes)

Read the results section while referring back to the figures. The text explains what the numbers mean. The figures show you the actual data. You need both.

Step 5 - Discussion and Conclusion (5 minutes)

This is where the authors tell you what they think their results mean. Read critically here because the discussion is interpretation, not fact. Ask whether they are overreaching and whether their conclusions actually follow from the data.

Step 6 - Methods (only if needed)

Most of the time you do not need to read the full methods section. Read it only if you want to replicate the study, you are confused about how a result was generated, or you are evaluating whether the methodology was sound.

Part 3: What to Do With Jargon

You will encounter words you do not know. Every researcher does, even in fields they have worked in for years. Here is the protocol:

  • Tier 1 - Skip it. If an unfamiliar word appears once and the surrounding sentence still makes sense, skip it. Not every term matters.
  • Tier 2 - Google it. For recurring terms that seem important, search the term plus "simple explanation." A 30-second search usually gives you enough to continue.
  • Tier 3 - Look it up in the paper itself. Many papers define key terms in the introduction. Search the word and check if it was defined earlier.
  • Tier 4 - Find a review article. If the jargon is dense throughout, search for a review paper on the same topic. Reviews are written to summarize a field for people entering it.

Never stop reading a paper just because you do not understand one word. Comprehension does not require understanding 100 percent of the vocabulary.

Part 4: Active Reading Questions

After the abstract: What is the research question? What is the single most important finding?

After the figures: What trend is the data showing? Does this support the abstract claim?

After the introduction: What gap in existing knowledge does this study fill?

After the results: Are the results statistically significant? Are there any results that contradict the main conclusion?

After the discussion: What limitations do the authors acknowledge? What would the logical next experiment be?

Part 5: The Five-Sentence Annotation

A useful paper annotation has five things. The question (one sentence). The method (two sentences max). The main finding (one sentence). One limitation (one sentence). One follow-up question it raised for you (one sentence). That is it. Five sentences. Use this template:

Paper: [Title, Authors, Year]
Journal:
Question:
Method:
Main finding:
Key limitation:
My follow-up question:

Python for Biology

Estimated time: 45-60 minutes  |  No prerequisites required

What you will be able to do after this

Write real Python code to load biological data, analyze it, and visualize it. The exact same foundation used in published biomedical research.

Why Python and Why Biology

Python is the dominant programming language in biomedical research. It is used for analyzing genomic data, processing medical images, building machine learning models that detect disease, and automating experiments. If you want to do computational biology, bioinformatics, or biomedical AI at any level, Python is the entry point.

Python was specifically designed to be readable. It looks closer to plain English than any other programming language. A loop that repeats something ten times looks like this:

for i in range(10):
    print(i)

This module teaches Python through biology. Every concept is introduced with a biological example. By the end you will have written code that does something meaningful with real data.

Part 1: Setting Up Your Environment

Option A - Google Colab (recommended for beginners)

Google Colab is a free browser-based coding environment. No installation required. Go to colab.research.google.com, sign in with a Google account, and click "New Notebook." You are ready to write Python immediately. This is what most student researchers use for machine learning and data analysis.

Option B - VS Code (for a professional setup)

Download VS Code from code.visualstudio.com, then download Python from python.org, and install the Python extension inside VS Code. This is what professional software engineers use and it is worth learning eventually, but Colab is faster to get started with today.

For this module, use Colab. Type each code block as you read it. Reading code without writing it is like reading a recipe without cooking.

Part 2: Variables, Print, and Math

patient_age = 67
diagnosis = "pneumonia"

heart_rate_morning = 72
heart_rate_evening = 85
average_heart_rate = (heart_rate_morning + heart_rate_evening) / 2
print(average_heart_rate)  # outputs 78.5

Three things to notice: numbers do not need quotes, text strings need quotes, and True/False booleans are capitalized with no quotes.

Part 3: Lists, Loops, and Conditionals

white_blood_cell_counts = [4500, 11200, 6800, 13400, 5100, 9200]

for count in white_blood_cell_counts:
    if count > 10000:
        print(count, "-- elevated, possible infection")
    else:
        print(count, "-- within normal range")

Normal white blood cell count is roughly 4,500 to 11,000 cells per microliter. This loop flags any value above 10,000. You just wrote a basic clinical screening rule in six lines of code.

Module 03

Medical Imaging and AI

Estimated time: 40-50 minutes  |  Module 1 recommended, Module 2 helpful

What you will be able to do after this

Understand what medical images actually are as data, how they are stored, how doctors read them, and how a neural network learns to find disease in an image. Including why AI sometimes gets it wrong and how researchers check for that.

Part 1: What Medical Images Actually Are

A digital image is a grid of pixels. Each pixel is a number representing brightness at that location. A chest X-ray loaded into Python is just a matrix of numbers that happens to represent anatomy when displayed visually. A grayscale image has one value per pixel, ranging from 0 (black) to 255 (white).

Why different tissues look different

X-rays work by passing radiation through the body. Dense tissues like bone absorb more radiation and appear white. Air absorbs almost none and appears black. Soft tissue falls in between. When pneumonia causes the air spaces in the lung to fill with fluid (called consolidation), that region becomes denser. It shows up as a white or gray patch where there should be dark lung tissue. That white patch is what a radiologist and a trained AI model both learn to look for.

The four main types

  • X-ray: Fast, cheap, widely available. Best for bones and lungs. Limitation: a 3D body compressed into a 2D image, which can obscure findings.
  • CT scan: Hundreds of X-rays reconstructed into a 3D volume. Far more detailed. Much higher radiation dose and cost.
  • MRI: Uses magnetic fields instead of radiation. Exceptional for soft tissue like brain and spinal cord. Slower and more expensive than CT.
  • Pathology slides: Tissue stained with dyes and digitized. AI models detecting cancer from pathology slides are among the most clinically advanced applications currently deployed.

DICOM format

In a hospital, medical images are stored as DICOM files, not JPEGs. A DICOM file contains the image plus metadata including patient age, sex, imaging date, and equipment settings. This metadata is how researchers analyze whether a model performs differently across demographic subgroups.

Part 2: How Neural Networks Learn to See

A convolutional neural network (CNN) for image classification learns to map an input image to an output label by adjusting millions of internal parameters based on examples.

Transfer learning

Training a CNN from scratch requires millions of labeled examples. Medical datasets rarely have that many. Transfer learning solves this by starting with a model already trained on a massive general image dataset like ImageNet, then fine-tuning it on your smaller medical dataset. This is why nearly every student medical AI project uses architectures like ResNet, VGG, or EfficientNet with ImageNet pretrained weights.

from tensorflow.keras.applications import ResNet50
from tensorflow.keras.layers import Dense, GlobalAveragePooling2D
from tensorflow.keras.models import Model

base_model = ResNet50(weights='imagenet', include_top=False, input_shape=(224, 224, 3))
base_model.trainable = False

x = base_model.output
x = GlobalAveragePooling2D()(x)
x = Dense(256, activation='relu')(x)
output = Dense(1, activation='sigmoid')(x)

model = Model(inputs=base_model.input, outputs=output)
model.compile(optimizer='adam', loss='binary_crossentropy', metrics=['accuracy'])

Part 3: Why Accuracy Is Not Enough

Suppose your test set has 700 normal cases and 300 pneumonia cases. A model that predicts "normal" for every single image achieves 70 percent accuracy but is completely useless clinically. It would miss every pneumonia case.

In medicine the consequences of different types of errors are not equal. Missing a pneumonia case in a vulnerable patient could mean they go untreated and deteriorate. These two types of errors have different clinical stakes and need to be measured separately.

The four metrics that matter

  • Sensitivity (Recall): Of all patients who actually have the disease, what fraction did the model correctly identify? This is the most clinically important metric for pneumonia detection.
  • Specificity: Of all healthy patients, what fraction did the model correctly identify as healthy?
  • AUC-ROC: A score from 0.5 to 1.0 measuring overall model discrimination. Above 0.9 is generally considered strong.
  • F1 Score: The harmonic mean of sensitivity and precision. Useful when your dataset is imbalanced.

A strong medical AI paper reports all four metrics. If you see a paper that only reports accuracy, that is a red flag.

Part 4: Grad-CAM and Explainability

Even a model with excellent sensitivity has a problem: you do not know why it is making its predictions. It could be detecting genuine pneumonia opacities in the lung fields. It could also be picking up on imaging artifacts from different hospitals. Multiple published papers have shown that medical AI models can achieve high accuracy by detecting scanner watermarks rather than actual pathology.

Grad-CAM produces a heatmap showing which regions of the input image most strongly influenced the model's prediction. If your pneumonia model is working correctly, the heatmap should highlight the lung fields where consolidation is present. If the heatmap is highlighting image borders or areas outside the lungs, your model has learned something spurious and your results are not trustworthy regardless of your accuracy numbers.

This is the step that elevates a student project into something publishable. Reporting Grad-CAM validation demonstrates scientific rigor and directly addresses the question every reviewer will ask: how do you know your model is detecting pneumonia rather than something else?

Part 5: Bias in Medical AI

Understanding bias is increasingly central to medical AI research and it is an area where student researchers can make genuine contributions.

  • Dataset bias: The model learns patterns specific to the data it was trained on that do not generalize to other settings.
  • Demographic bias: The model performs differently across patient subgroups by age, sex, or race. A pneumonia AI trained predominantly on adult images may perform significantly worse on pediatric chest X-rays because the anatomy looks different.
  • Label bias: Medical image labels in research datasets are often generated by one radiologist. A model trained on one radiologist's labels inherits that radiologist's blind spots.

If your dataset has demographic metadata, you can directly measure whether your model's performance varies across subgroups. If you find meaningful differences in AUC across subgroups, that is a genuine scientific finding and the kind of result that gets published in journals focused on AI fairness and health equity.

Module 04

Getting Your First Lab Placement

Estimated time: 35-45 minutes  |  No prerequisites required

What you will be able to do after this

Write a cold email that actually gets responses, identify realistic research targets, navigate formal programs versus independent placements, and build a research profile that compounds over time.

Why Most Students Never Get Research Experience

It is not because they are not smart enough and it is not because they do not have the right connections. It is because they make one of three mistakes: they aim too high too early, they write emails about themselves instead of about the research, or they give up after two rejections and conclude that research is not accessible to them. All three mistakes are fixable.

Part 1: Formal Programs vs. Independent Lab Placements

There are two fundamentally different paths to research experience and most students only know about one of them.

Formal programs are structured, application-based, often competitive, and come with built-in mentorship and defined timelines. They have branding that looks good on a resume.

Independent lab placements are arrangements you make directly with a professor. No application portal, no central deadline. You email someone, they say yes, you show up and do science. These are less well-known among students but are in many ways more valuable because the relationship is direct and the connection to the PI is personal rather than institutional.

Most students compete intensely for formal programs. Far fewer cold email labs directly, which means your competition for an independent placement is dramatically lower.

Program tier list

  • Tier 1 (life-changing): RSI (Research Science Institute) at MIT. Six weeks, fully funded, original research with a faculty mentor. Admission rate roughly 1 to 2 percent. A single RSI signals Ivy-level research ability before admissions reads anything else.
  • Tier 2 (genuinely strong): SEAP places students in real DoD labs. Paid, full-time, eight weeks, real scientist as your direct supervisor. MIT PRIMES for math and CS. COSMOS and similar university programs.
  • Tier 3 (solid but not differentiating alone): Pre-college programs at Hopkins, Northwestern, and similar. Yale YGS and similar branded programs. Good for intellectual enrichment but do not signal research depth.

Part 2: Finding the Right Labs to Target

The single most important factor in cold email success is targeting the right people. A perfectly written email to the wrong person gets no response.

What makes a PI the right person

  • Their research overlaps with what you can actually contribute. Think about what you bring to the table, not just what you want to learn.
  • They have active funding. A PI with an active R01 grant from NIH has money, has ongoing projects, and needs people to work on them. Check at reporter.nih.gov.
  • They supervise students at your level. Look at the "People" page on their lab website. If you see high school alumni listed, that PI has a precedent for taking students like you.

Where to find labs

  • NIH Reporter at reporter.nih.gov: Search by keyword and filter by state. Every result is an actively funded lab.
  • University department websites: Go to the biomedical engineering or biology department at any nearby research university. Click on faculty whose research descriptions match your interests.
  • Google Scholar: Search for a topic that interests you and look at who is publishing in that space. Most cited recent authors are the most active researchers.

Part 3: The Cold Email Formula

The emails that work are about the PI's research, not the student's resume. A cold email should be five to seven sentences maximum. Here is the structure:

  • Sentence 1: Who you are in one line. Your name, school, grade, and the most relevant thing about your current work. Not your GPA. Your research.
  • Sentence 2: Something specific about their work. Reference a specific paper or finding. This sentence is the most important one. It proves you read something and that you are not mass-emailing.
  • Sentence 3: The connection. Connect their work to something you are already doing or thinking about.
  • Sentence 4: A specific, low-commitment ask. Not "I want to join your lab." Something like "I would welcome the chance to learn more about your work and explore whether there is a way I could contribute."
  • Sentence 5: Offer to send your resume or GitHub. Gives them a low-effort next step if they are interested.

The subject line format that works: "High School Researcher at [Your Lab/School] - Interest in [Their Specific Field]"

Following up

Wait ten to fourteen days after your initial email. Send one follow-up, shorter than the original, referencing that you sent a previous email and are still interested. After one follow-up with no response, move on. Your time is better spent contacting new people.

Cold emailing is a numbers game. A response rate of 10 to 20 percent is normal. Send ten to fifteen emails to different labs staggered over two to three weeks. Keep a simple tracking spreadsheet with columns for PI name, institution, date sent, follow-up date, response, and status.

Part 4: Once You Are In

Your only goal in the first two weeks is to demonstrate that you are reliable and easy to work with. Do not try to make a big impression with clever ideas. Do exactly what you are asked, do it thoroughly, and do it on time.

At a natural moment after you have delivered your first substantial piece of work, ask your supervisor whether there is a path toward publication for the project you are working on. This is a completely normal conversation to have and it signals ambition and follow-through, which are exactly the qualities that make a PI want to invest in you.

The most common mistake students make after a summer placement is disappearing in September. Send a brief email update to your supervisor every six to eight weeks during the school year. Three sentences. What you have been working on, one thing you learned, and a note that you are still interested in continuing the work.

Module 05

From Research to Publication

Estimated time: 45-55 minutes  |  Modules 1 and 4 recommended

What you will be able to do after this

Understand the full publication pipeline, write each section of a scientific paper correctly, navigate peer review, and identify the right journals for your work as a high school or early undergraduate researcher.

Is Your Research Publishable?

Three questions every publishable paper must answer: Does it address a real scientific question? Does the methodology support the conclusions? Does it add something to what is already known?

For a first publication in a student-focused journal, the bar is not groundbreaking discovery. It is rigorous process and genuine contribution, however small. A paper that carefully applies a well-established method to a new dataset, honestly evaluates its limitations, and clearly articulates what was learned is publishable.

Part 1: The Journal Landscape

Peer-reviewed student journals

  • Journal of Emerging Investigators (JEI): The most well-known peer-reviewed journal specifically for middle and high school researchers. Free to submit, rigorous peer review by graduate students and postdocs. Turnaround typically two to four months. This is the primary target for most high school research papers in the life sciences.
  • Cureus: A peer-reviewed medical journal that accepts work from early-career researchers. More clinical in focus than JEI.

Preprint servers

A preprint is a paper posted publicly before peer review. It is not peer-reviewed but it establishes a public timestamp for your work and makes it immediately discoverable. bioRxiv is for biology and biomedical research. medRxiv is for clinical and health sciences. arXiv is for computational and AI-focused research. Posting a preprint while your paper is under review at a journal is standard practice in most fields.

Avoid predatory journals. The signal that a journal is predatory is an unsolicited email inviting you to submit and a processing fee without a corresponding reputation in the field. Publishing in a predatory journal damages your credibility. When in doubt, check if the journal is indexed on PubMed.

Part 2: Writing Each Section

Title

Write the title last. A good title communicates three things: what was studied, what approach was used, and what was found. "Deep Learning Detection of Pneumonia" is too vague. "Transfer Learning-Based Detection of Pediatric Pneumonia from Chest Radiographs Reveals Significant Performance Disparities Compared to Adult Cohorts" is specific, informative, and honest about the main finding.

Abstract

A self-contained summary of the entire paper in 150 to 300 words. Must include the research question, the dataset and methods, the main results with specific numbers, and the conclusion. "We achieved high accuracy" is not an abstract finding. "Our model achieved 94.3 percent sensitivity and 0.923 AUC-ROC on the held-out test set" is. Write the abstract last.

Introduction

Three jobs: establish the clinical context, review relevant prior work, and state the specific aim of your study. Every claim about prior work needs a citation. End with one to three sentences stating explicitly what your study does.

Methods

The methods section has one job: enable replication. A reader who wants to repeat your experiment should be able to do so using only this section. Report your dataset with total samples and class distribution, preprocessing steps with specific numbers, model architecture with pretrained weights and which layers were frozen, training details including optimizer and learning rate, all evaluation metrics with their formulas, and your interpretability method if you used Grad-CAM.

Results

Reports what you found. No interpretation. Lead with your primary results in a table. Include confusion matrix, subgroup analysis if you have it, and Grad-CAM figures for representative examples.

Discussion

Restate the main finding in one sentence (the meaning, not the numbers), explain why, compare to prior work, state your limitations honestly, and suggest one to two future directions. The limitations section is the one students most want to skip and least should. A student who identifies their own limitations before being prompted demonstrates scientific maturity.

Conclusion

Two to four sentences maximum. Restate what you did, what you found, and why it matters. Do not introduce new information.

Part 3: Peer Review

When you submit to JEI, your paper is assigned to two or three peer reviewers who return written comments. The possible outcomes are accept as is (extremely rare), minor revisions, major revisions, or reject.

Major revisions are not rejection. They are an invitation to fix specific problems and resubmit. Most papers that eventually get published went through major revisions. When you receive comments, prepare a point-by-point response document listing every comment and your response. For every change you made, describe what you changed. For every point where you disagreed, explain your reasoning politely and specifically.

Part 4: Authorship and Ethics

Have an explicit conversation with your supervisor about authorship before you begin writing. Each author must have made substantial contributions to the work, participated in drafting or revising the manuscript, and approved the final version.

Never fabricate or manipulate data. Never report results on data that was used during model training. Your test set must be completely isolated from your training process.

Use AI tools to check grammar or get unstuck on a single sentence, not to generate sections. The writing process forces you to clarify your own thinking in ways that reading AI-generated text does not.

Try It Yourself

Assignment 1

Take any research project you have worked on and write one paragraph answering each of the three publishability questions. Be honest. If the answer to any question is no, write one sentence about what would need to change to make it yes.

Assignment 2

Write a methods section for your most technically complete project. Aim for 400 to 600 words. Check it against the replication standard: could someone recreate your exact experiment from this text alone?

Assignment 3

Download Zotero for free at zotero.org. Install the browser extension. Open five papers you have read in your research area and save them with one click each. Set your citation style to AMA or Vancouver. You now have a reference manager. Use it for every paper you read from this point forward.

Module 06

Building Your Professional Profile

Estimated time: 50-60 minutes  |  No prerequisites required

What you will be able to do after this

Build a LinkedIn profile that gets noticed, set up a professional email signature, create a personal research website, write a clean resume, and understand what colleges are actually looking for in extracurriculars so you stop collecting activities and start building a story.

What Selective Colleges Actually Want

Most students approach high school extracurriculars as a collection problem. They try to accumulate as many activities as possible under the assumption that more equals better. Admissions officers at selective universities have been explicit, repeatedly and publicly, that this is wrong.

A student with five deep, connected extracurriculars and one publication is more compelling than a student with ten shallow activities and nothing to show for any of them. What selective universities want is a class that is collectively well-rounded, made up of students who are individually spiky: deeply excellent at one or two things rather than moderately good at many things.

When you think about your ten Common App activity slots, ask this about each one: does this activity deepen my story, or does it just fill a slot?

Part 1: LinkedIn

Headline

Your headline appears under your name everywhere on LinkedIn. Most students write "Student at [School]" which communicates nothing. Write something that communicates your research identity and your current work. Example: "Biomedical AI Research | Microfluidics and Diagnostics | CUA Research Intern | Founder of NeuroSage"

About section

Two to three paragraphs in first person. First paragraph establishes who you are and what you work on. Second goes deeper on your current research. Third states what you are looking for. Be concrete about what you actually do. Do not use buzzwords without specifics behind them.

Experience section

For each position, write two to three bullet points describing what you actually did. Use numbers and specifics wherever possible.

Weak: "Conducted research on machine learning and medical imaging"

Strong: "Built a Python pipeline using OpenCV and SciPy to extract flow features from high-speed microfluidic imaging data; contributed analysis to a co-authored manuscript in preparation with Dr. Christopher Raub, Catholic University of America"

Projects section

Add every substantive project with a description and a link to your GitHub repository, your published paper DOI, your preprint, or your blog post about the project. This turns your LinkedIn profile into a portfolio, not just a resume.

Part 2: Email Signature

Every email you send to a professor or program director should include a signature. Include your name, your school and graduation year, your current research role, your email, your LinkedIn URL, and your GitHub URL.

Andrew Matelis
Student Researcher, Biomedical Engineering | Catholic University of America
Gonzaga College High School '28
Email: matelisa@gonzaga.org
LinkedIn: linkedin.com/in/andrewmatelis
GitHub: github.com/AMatelis

Set this up in Gmail Settings under Signatures. Takes five minutes and you never have to think about it again.

Part 3: Personal Research Website

A personal website gives professors, program directors, and admissions officers a single place to see everything: research, writing, code, publications. What your website needs:

  • A clear header with your name and one-line description
  • An About section (two to three paragraphs)
  • A Research section listing every project with descriptions and links
  • A Publications section with every paper and DOI link
  • A Writing or Blog section linking to your blog
  • A CV or Resume download as a PDF
  • Contact information

Build it for free on Google Sites, GitHub Pages, or Carrd. Then spend $10 to $15 per year on a custom domain at Namecheap. andrewmatelis.com looks more professional than andrewmatelis.github.io and the cost is trivial.

A minimal site that is complete and accurate is better than an ambitious site that is half-finished. Set a two-hour time limit and publish whatever you have at the end of it.

Part 4: Resume Bullet Points

Every bullet point on your resume should follow this structure: action verb plus what you did plus result or scale.

Weak: "Helped with machine learning research"

Strong: "Developed Python pipeline using OpenCV and SciPy to extract 12 flow features from high-speed microfluidic imaging data, contributing to a manuscript in preparation"

Every bullet should make a skeptical reader think "I believe this person actually did this."

Part 5: GitHub as Your Code Portfolio

If you do any computational research, GitHub is not optional. Every repository should have a README that explains what the project does, what technologies it uses, how to run it, and what the results were. GitHub displays a contribution graph showing how often you commit code. A profile with consistent activity over months tells a story of sustained engagement. Pin your best and most relevant repositories to the top of your profile.

Try It Yourself

Assignment 1

Write your LinkedIn headline and a two-paragraph About section right now. Read it back and ask: does this sound like a specific person or could it have been written by anyone?

Assignment 2

Set up your email signature in Gmail today. Five minutes, done forever.

Assignment 3

Build a one-page website this weekend. Choose Google Sites for speed, GitHub Pages for control, or Carrd for design. Set a two-hour time limit and publish whatever you have at the end of it.

Assignment 4 - The story test

Lay out all your current extracurriculars in a list. Read them as if you are an admissions officer who knows nothing about you. Ask: do these activities tell a coherent story about one person with a clear identity, or do they read as a collection of impressive-sounding things with no through line? Write one sentence that connects everything on your list. If you cannot write that sentence, you have identified the strategic gap in your application.

Module 07

How to Place in a Science Competition

Estimated time: 25-30 minutes  |  Module 5 helpful but not required

What you will be able to do after this

Understand how science competition judging actually works, identify the right competitions for your research, frame your work for maximum impact, and avoid the mistakes that keep good research from placing.

Why Science Competitions Matter

A publication tells admissions officers your research is real. A competition placement tells them your research is good. These are different signals and both matter. A Regeneron STS scholar designation or an ISEF finalist placement is one of the few credentials in a high school application that is both externally validated and genuinely rare.

Part 1: The Competition Landscape

Tier 1 - Most prestigious

  • Regeneron Science Talent Search (STS): The oldest and most prestigious science competition in the United States. Open to US high school seniors. You submit an independent research paper in November of senior year. 300 scholars selected, then 40 finalists invited to Washington DC. Awards up to $250,000.
  • Intel / Regeneron ISEF: The largest pre-college science competition in the world. You must qualify through a regional or state science fair. ISEF is often the pipeline for STS. Your ISEF project is what you refine and submit to STS the following year.

Tier 2 - Highly respected and more accessible

  • Junior Science and Humanities Symposium (JSHS): Sponsored by the US Army, Navy, and Air Force. Significant scholarships for winners. Strong in physical sciences and engineering.
  • Davidson Fellows Scholarship: Not a traditional competition. You submit a project and are selected as a Fellow. Awards of $10,000, $25,000, and $50,000. Exceptionally competitive.
  • Breakthrough Junior Challenge: Video-based competition where you explain a complex scientific concept. $250,000 scholarship for the winner. Rewards science communication rather than original research.

Other competitions worth knowing

  • Science Olympiad and Science Bowl: Team-based competitions excellent for building scientific breadth before you have independent research to enter.
  • AMC / AIME / USAMO: The mathematics competition pipeline. USAMO qualification alone is a significant credential for selective university applications.
  • Biology Olympiad (USABO), Chemistry Olympiad (USNCO), Physics Olympiad (USAPhO): Subject-specific pipelines with national and international levels.
  • Genes in Space: Competition specifically for genetics research proposals. Winners conduct experiments aboard the International Space Station.

Part 2: How Judging Actually Works

Most students focus entirely on the quality of their research and almost none on how it is communicated and framed. Both matter. At ISEF and most major competitions, judges interview you directly at your project display for ten to twenty minutes. This interview is where placements are determined more than any other factor.

What judges are looking for

  • Creative ability: Did you ask an original question? Did you design your own experiment?
  • Scientific thought: Do you understand the limitations of your work? Can you explain why you chose this method over alternatives?
  • Thoroughness: Is the work complete? Incomplete projects with promising preliminary results place significantly lower than complete projects with modest results.
  • Clarity: Can you explain your research to someone who is not in your field? This is explicitly evaluated by judges, many of whom will not be specialists in your area.

Prepare for these six questions

  • What is the central question your research addresses? Answer in one sentence without jargon.
  • Why is this question important?
  • Why did you choose this specific approach and what alternatives did you consider?
  • What are the limitations of your work?
  • What would you do next if you could continue this research?
  • What was the most surprising finding?

Practice answering all six questions out loud before the competition. Not in your head. Out loud, to another person. The difference between having the answer in your head and delivering it clearly under mild pressure from a stranger is significant and only rehearsal closes that gap.

Part 3: The Competition Timeline

  • Sophomore and junior fall: Register for your regional science fair at societyforscience.org/regional-affiliated-fairs.
  • Junior summer: Your primary research window. The work you do this summer becomes your competition entry.
  • Junior fall (November): AMC 10 or 12 competition. Enter JSHS regional competition.
  • Junior fall (January through March): Compete at your regional science fair. Top placements qualify you for state and potentially ISEF.
  • Senior fall (November): Submit to Regeneron STS using your junior summer research. This is your highest-stakes submission.

Part 4: The Single Most Common Reason Good Research Does Not Place

Students who do genuinely excellent research and do not place well at competitions almost always share one failure: they present their work as a technical accomplishment rather than a scientific contribution.

A judge does not care that you trained a model with 94 percent accuracy. They care whether that model answered a question that matters and whether the researcher in front of them understands the implications of what they found.

Instead of: "I built a deep learning model that detects pneumonia with 94 percent accuracy"

Say: "I investigated whether AI-assisted pneumonia detection performs equitably across pediatric and adult patients, because if diagnostic AI works well for adults but fails for children, deploying it could widen existing health disparities rather than close them. What I found was a statistically significant performance gap that prior work in this space had not examined."

Same research. Completely different impression. Lead with your scientific question and why it matters every time.

Try It Yourself

Assignment 1

Go to societyforscience.org/regional-affiliated-fairs and find the fair that covers your school. Write down the registration deadline, the project submission deadline, and the fair date. Put all three in your calendar right now.

Assignment 2

Take any research project you have worked on and write a one-sentence answer to the question "what is the central question your research addresses?" No jargon, no methodology, no results. Just the question and why it matters. If it takes more than one sentence, it is not clear enough yet.

Assignment 3

Rewrite your current research description using the framing from Part 4. Lead with the scientific question and its significance. Read both versions back. The difference in how they land is the difference between a project that places and one that does not.
